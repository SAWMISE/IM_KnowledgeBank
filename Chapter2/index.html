International Trade Theories

Classical (Country-Based) Theories

1500s

Mercantilism

Countries should maximize exports and minimize imports to accumulate gold and silver wealth. Led to protectionist policies and colonial expansion.

Wealth = Gold + Silver
1776

Absolute Advantage

Adam Smith argued countries should specialize in producing goods they can make more efficiently than others, promoting free trade.

Specialization + Efficiency
1817

Comparative Advantage

David Ricardo showed that even without absolute advantage, countries benefit by specializing in what they do relatively better.

Relative Productivity
1900s

Heckscher-Ohlin Theory

Countries export goods using abundant factors (land, labor, capital) and import goods requiring scarce factors.

Factor Proportions

Modern (Firm-Based) Theories

Country Similarity Theory

Trade occurs between countries with similar development stages and consumer preferences.

Steffan Linder (1961)

Product Life Cycle Theory

Products move through stages (new, maturing, standardized) affecting production location.

Raymond Vernon (1960s)

Global Strategic Rivalry

Firms compete globally by developing barriers to entry through R&D, IP rights, and scale.

Krugman & Lancaster (1980s)

Porter's Diamond

National competitiveness depends on four determinants: factor conditions, demand, supporting industries, and firm characteristics.

Michael Porter (1990)

Government Trade Intervention

Why Governments Intervene:

  • Political protection of jobs and industries
  • National security considerations
  • Economic development goals
  • Cultural and social preservation
  • Retaliation or reward mechanisms

How Governments Intervene:

Tariffs

Taxes on imports to protect domestic industries

Subsidies

Government payments to domestic producers

Import Quotas

Limits on quantity of imports allowed

Currency Controls

Managing exchange rates to affect trade

Local Content Requirements

Mandating percentage of domestic production

Free Trade Zones

Areas with reduced trade restrictions

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI)

Types of International Investment

Portfolio Investment

Investment in stocks, bonds, or assets without operational control

  • Financial return focused
  • Risk diversification
  • No management control
VS

Foreign Direct Investment

Investment with intent to control and manage operations

  • Long-term strategy
  • Operational control
  • Market access goals

Forms of FDI

By Direction

Horizontal FDI

Opening new markets (e.g., retailer expanding abroad)

Vertical FDI

Supporting core operations

  • Backward: Securing supply inputs
  • Forward: Accessing distribution channels

By Implementation

Greenfield FDI

Building new facilities from scratch

  • Custom-built facilities
  • New job creation
  • Higher initial investment
Brownfield FDI

Purchasing/leasing existing facilities

  • Faster implementation
  • Lower costs
  • Existing challenges

Government FDI Policies

Encouraging FDI

  • Financial incentives and tax breaks
  • Infrastructure development
  • Streamlined administrative processes
  • Education and workforce development
  • Political and economic stability

Restricting FDI

  • Ownership restrictions
  • Higher tax rates and penalties
  • Complex bureaucratic processes
  • Local partner requirements
  • Industry-specific limitations

Case Studies

China in Africa

Resource Diplomacy

China has invested nearly $14 billion in African resource deals since 2004, using resource-backed loans to gain access to oil, minerals, and commodities while funding infrastructure development.

Strategy

  • Oil-backed loans to countries like Angola and Nigeria
  • Chinese companies build infrastructure (roads, railways, hospitals)
  • Loans repaid in resources rather than cash
  • Government-level partnerships for long-term access

Impact

  • $106.8 billion in China-Africa trade (2008)
  • Chinese companies present in 48 African countries
  • Infrastructure development across the continent
  • Criticism over human rights and local employment

Hong Kong's Rise

Trade Hub Success

Hong Kong's openness to FDI and international trade transformed it from a colonial port to a global financial center, demonstrating how pro-trade policies drive economic growth.

Success Factors:

  • Business-friendly laws and policies
  • Strategic geographic location
  • Open investment structure
  • English-language business environment
  • Gateway to mainland China

China's Solar Industry

State Capitalism

Government intervention created a dominant global solar industry through targeted investments, favorable loans, and rapid approvals for domestic manufacturers.

Government Support:

  • $1 billion funding to companies like GCL-Poly
  • Quick loans with favorable terms
  • Speedy regulatory approvals
  • 25% market share in polysilicon achieved
